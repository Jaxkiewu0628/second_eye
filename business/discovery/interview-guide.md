# Second Eye — Discovery Call Interview Guide

_The goal of these calls is to settle one question before we build: is the hero feature **mobility** (knowing what's around you while walking) or **information access** (reading text, recognizing things)? The market research could not answer it. Real users will._

## The 3 rules (read before every call)
1. **Do not pitch.** The moment you describe Second Eye, they start being polite. Learn first. If they ask what you're building, say "I'm trying to understand the problem before I build anything" and steer back.
2. **The status quo is the competitor.** What they already do today — cane, phone, asking people, memorized routes — is what we have to beat. Map it in detail.
3. **Specific beats general.** Push past "it's hard sometimes" to "last Tuesday I hit a tree branch at the bus stop and cut my forehead." Stories, not opinions. Ask "tell me about the last time..." not "do you usually..."

## Who to interview
- 8–10 blind / low-vision adults. Get a MIX on purpose: totally blind AND low-vision; white-cane users AND guide-dog users; tech-comfortable AND not; a range of ages. The answer may differ by group — that itself is signal.
- 3 orientation-and-mobility (O&M) instructors. They watch dozens of blind people travel; they see patterns no single user can.

## The script (about 30 minutes)

**1. Warm-up (2 min).** "Thanks for the time. I'm not selling anything. I'm trying to learn how you get around and get information day to day, so I can understand where the real friction is. Mind if I record audio just for my notes?"

**2. Their world today (8 min). Map the status quo. → Block 2 (abandonment predictors) + the status-quo competitor.**
- "Walk me through getting somewhere unfamiliar yesterday or this week. Start to finish."
- "What tools did you use? Cane, phone, apps, a person?"
- "Tell me about the last time travel went badly. What happened?"
- **Device graveyard (Block 2):** "What assistive devices or apps have you bought and then *stopped* using? What made you quit — comfort, battery, too slow, didn't trust it, blocked your hearing?" _(Past abandonment is the single best predictor of ours.)_
- "What about reading something — a menu, mail, a label — when did you last need to and how did you handle it?"
- _Listen for: where the time and stress actually go. Don't categorize for them yet._

**3. The wedge probe (8 min). Mobility vs. information — without tipping our hand. → Block 5 (segment / buyer fit).**
Ask this neutrally so we don't plant the answer:
- "If you could wave a wand and have one thing about getting around or getting information just... handled, what would it be?" (open, let them choose the category)
- Then force a trade-off: "Two magic glasses exist. Glasses A quietly tells you what's in your path and around you as you walk — steps, poles, people, a low branch. Glasses B reads any text aloud and tells you what it sees — signs, menus, faces, products. You can only have one. Which, and why?"
- "What would make you pick the other one instead?"
- _This is the single most important question. Capture the why verbatim. Watch for hesitation, not just the pick._

**4. The free-app reality check (5 min). → Block 3 (WTP vs the free floor).**
- "Do you use Seeing AI, Be My Eyes, Aira, or Lookout? Which, and for what?"
- "When do they work great? When do they fall down or annoy you?"
- "Has holding up a phone ever been the problem — hands full, raining, walking, looked awkward?"
- _We must beat free. This tells us if hands-free + offline + instant is actually worth paying for._

**5. Wearability + trust (4 min). → Block 1 (trust), Block 6 (coexistence + stigma).**
- "Would you wear glasses on your face all day? Any reason you wouldn't?" (some reject face-wear; guide-dog users have strong views)
- "If a device told you the path was clear and it was wrong, once — would you ever trust it again? What would it take?"
- "Would this replace your cane, or sit alongside it?" (correct answer is almost always alongside)
- **Ears-open check (Block 1/2):** "When you're walking, how much do you rely on *hearing* — traffic, echoes, footsteps? Would audio in your ears bother you?" _(Validates the ears-open / bone-conduction choice; audio that blocks residual senses drives abandonment.)_

**6. Who pays (3 min). → Block 3 (WTP), Block 4 (reimbursement). For the VA/VR funder calls, see `risk-blocks.md` Block 4.**
- "Have you ever gotten an assistive device paid for by the VA, voc-rehab, a state agency, insurance, or a charity? How did that work?"
- "If something like this were $400–700 one time, no subscription — is that something you'd buy yourself, or only if an agency covered it?"

**7. Wrap (1 min).** "Most useful thing you've told me is ___. Anyone else I should talk to?" (referrals compound.)

## What to listen for
- **Painkiller signal:** unprompted emotion, a recent specific incident, a workaround they built themselves, time/money spent. That's demand.
- **Vitamin signal:** "that'd be nice," "interesting," "I guess I'd use it." Discount heavily.
- **The surprise:** anything they do or want that contradicts our assumptions. Write it down in their words — that's often the real product.

## Red flags in our own behavior
- We talked more than 30% of the time → we pitched, start over.
- Everyone agreed with us → we asked leading questions.
- No one told a specific story → we stayed too abstract.

## Decision after ~10 calls
Tally the A-vs-B picks by group and read the *whys*. If mobility wins clearly → build obstacle-first. If reading wins → reading-first hero, obstacles as differentiator. If it splits by group → we have a segmentation choice to make, not a single wedge. Either way, we commit the spec on evidence, not the desk.

# Reimbursement & Funding-Pathway Research — Second Eye

> **Purpose:** Verify or refute the payer path we currently *assert* ("the VA provides electronic mobility devices and voc-rehab funds smart glasses"). This is make-or-break for go-to-market.
> **Researcher:** CEO research pass. **Date:** 2026-06-30. All claims tagged VERIFIED (primary or strong secondary source) or UNCONFIRMED (needs a call).
> **Bottom line up front:** The VA path is **REAL and is the single most promising mechanism.** Medicare/Medicaid is **a dead end (myth)** for our device. State voc-rehab is **real but employment-gated.** FSA/HSA is real but small. See the executive summary at the end and `## Sources` for URLs.

## Q1 — HCPCS/CPT codes & whether Medicare covers low-vision/vision-mobility aids

**VERIFIED — Medicare broadly EXCLUDES low-vision aids, including electronic ones.** This refutes any assumption that we can bill Medicare.

- **The statutory "eyeglass" exclusion.** The Social Security Act excludes eyeglasses and refractive vision aids from Medicare. CMS has interpreted this *broadly* to cover devices "irrespective of their size, form, or technological features that use one or more lens to aid vision or provide magnification of images for impaired vision." Advocacy groups (AFB, ITEM Coalition) argue Congress only meant traditional eyeglasses, but **the exclusion stands as current CMS posture.** (AFB "Low Vision Aid Exclusion"; AFB Medicare/Medicaid page.)
- **Low-vision aid codes are denied.** HCPCS **V2600 / V2610 / V2615** (low/near vision aids, spectacle-mounted aids) are denied as non-covered because the Medicare prosthetic benefit for lenses is limited to people with **congenital absence or surgical removal of the lens** (i.e., post-cataract). Electronic magnifiers and CCTVs are classed as "quality-of-life"/convenience items and not covered. (AAPC V2600–V2615; CMS Refractive Lenses Policy Article A52499; ALJ decision CR1603.)
- **The only routine exception:** one pair of glasses or contacts after cataract surgery with an intraocular lens implant. Not relevant to us.
- **E1399 (DME, miscellaneous).** VERIFIED as a real, valid 2026 code for DME that has no specific code — used for new-technology / custom items. It requires a physician's written order, a **Letter of Medical Necessity**, manufacturer/model/price documentation, and usually **prior authorization.** **CAVEAT (important):** E1399 is a *billing vehicle*, not a coverage guarantee. For a vision aid, the claim still runs into the low-vision-aid/eyeglass exclusion above, so E1399 does **not** rescue Medicare coverage for our category. It matters mainly if a *private* plan or voc-rehab/VA wants a code to process a claim. (hcpcs.codes E1399; AAPC E1399; CMS DMEPOS.)
- **Medicare DOES cover a small amount of vision *rehabilitation services* (not devices):** a demonstration allowed up to ~12 hours/year of in-home low-vision rehab. Services, not hardware — does not help device sales. (AFB Medicare/Medicaid page.)

**Net:** There is **no Medicare billing path for a Second Eye-type device.** Treating Medicare as a payer would be a strategic error. (UNCONFIRMED detail to ask a biller: whether any Medicare Advantage plan offers a supplemental low-vision-device benefit — Original Medicare does not.)

## Q2 — Does the VA actually issue electronic mobility devices to blind veterans?

**VERIFIED — YES. This is the strongest, most concrete finding, and it confirms our assertion almost verbatim.**

- **VA.gov says it in our exact words.** The official VA blind/low-vision rehab page states the VA provides "**vision-enhancing devices and technologies (like electronic reading machines and electronic mobility devices)**" plus training, **at no cost** to eligible veterans. (va.gov/health-care/.../blind-low-vision-rehab-services.)
- **The mechanism / process is documented:**
  1. Veteran contacts the **VIST (Visual Impairment Services Team) coordinator** at their VA medical center — the case manager and entry point, providing lifetime care coordination. (rehab.va.gov VIST fact sheet; VA.gov VIST pages.)
  2. **Blind Rehabilitation Service (BRS)** / Blind Rehabilitation Centers (BRCs) assess and prescribe; orientation-and-mobility specialists are involved. (rehab.va.gov About BRS.)
  3. **VA Prosthetic & Sensory Aids Service (PSAS)** issues approved equipment **at no cost to the patient.** Devices are categorized as **"Prosthetic and Sensory Aids."** (rehab.va.gov About BRS; rehab.va.gov About PSAS.)
- **Procurement is via the Federal Supply Schedule (FSS).** The VA's National Acquisition Center runs medical FSS schedules (FSC 65/66) through which it buys devices/sensory aids. **Getting on the VA FSS is the supplier-side action item for us.** (department.va.gov FSS; va.gov OPAL NAC.)
- **Documented device precedent (the keystone):** **eSight** (a $9,995 wearable low-vision display) is **VA-approved on FSS and issued through Blind Rehabilitation Centers** (e.g., Edward Hines Jr. VA Hospital), with the **VA covering 100%** of cost for qualifying registered veterans; the VA categorizes it under "Prosthetic and Sensory Aids." **IrisVision** likewise reports being "fully covered by the VA" for qualifying veterans, while *not* covered by Medicare/Medicaid/insurance. This is direct proof the VA buys modern wearable vision devices that Medicare refuses. (esighteyewear.com Veteran Coverage [via search snippet — page blocks direct fetch]; usveteransmagazine.com eSight/Marathon Medical; irisvision.com FAQ.)
- **38 CFR Part 17** is the regulatory basis for VA prosthetic/rehabilitative items, and notably its "cognitive device" language already contemplates navigation tech ("GPS"/navigating surroundings), showing the VA's device scope reaches mobility/navigation, not just magnifiers. (eCFR 38 CFR Part 17.)

**UNCONFIRMED — needs a VIST/Prosthetics call:**
- Has the VA issued **dedicated mobility/ETA devices** in our exact niche — **WeWALK, .lumen, BuzzClip, Sunu Band, Aira**? I found the VA's *category* ("electronic mobility devices") and a strong *display-device* precedent (eSight/IrisVision), but **no primary source naming WeWALK/.lumen/Aira as VA-issued.** (Note: BuzzClip and Sunu Band are now discontinued products.) The precedent I can prove is for low-vision *display* wearables; our obstacle-alert wearable is adjacent but not identically precedented. **Ask directly.**
- Does a veteran need a **service-connected** vision disability, or does any enrolled veteran with qualifying low vision/blindness qualify? (Eligibility nuance — confirm.)

## Q3 — State Vocational Rehabilitation (voc-rehab): can a counselor fund smart glasses?

**VERIFIED — YES, but it is employment-gated.** This partially confirms our assertion ("voc-rehab funds smart glasses") with an important catch.

- **Legal/program basis:** Voc-rehab is federally funded (RSA), state-administered, under the **Rehabilitation Act of 1973** and **WIOA**. The funding vehicle is the **Individualized Plan for Employment (IPE)** — a legal document listing the vocational goal, services, and equipment. (nelowvision.com VR funding; floridareading.com VR guides.)
- **What VR can buy:** evaluations, **assistive technology / devices**, software, braille/access tech (JAWS, NVDA named), **smart glasses**, OCR readers, note-takers, orientation-and-mobility services, job coaching. Secondary sources explicitly list **head-mounted displays like eSight/Eyedaptic and AI wearables such as OrCam and Envision** as fundable when tied to job demands. (nelowvision.com; floridareading.com; helpsquad.com.)
- **The catch (VERIFIED):** the client must have a **work goal** — currently employed, job-seeking, changing careers, returning to work, or a student preparing to enter employment — and the device must be justified as **essential to preparing for/getting/keeping a job** in the IPE. A retiree or someone not pursuing employment generally **doesn't qualify.** Typical timeline: **3–6 months** from first contact to device in hand; eligibility decision within 60 days. (nelowvision.com; floridareading.com.)

**UNCONFIRMED — needs a VR-counselor call:**
- Will a counselor fund a **mobility/obstacle-detection** device on the theory that *safe independent travel to and at work* is an employment necessity — or do they only fund **task/reading** devices (which is what the named precedents, OrCam/Envision/eSight, all are)? Our mobility framing is less precedented in VR than reading devices. **Ask directly.**
- Per-device **dollar caps** and whether they require an AT evaluation from an approved evaluator first.

## Q4 — Private precedent: how do OrCam, Envision, eSight, IrisVision actually get paid?

**VERIFIED — the realistic blend is: out-of-pocket + financing, VA (for vets), voc-rehab (for workers), FSA/HSA, and nonprofit/crowdfunding. NOT private insurance, NOT Medicare.** This is the single most useful reality check in this report.

- **eSight** ($9,995, down from $15,000): FDA- and EUDAMED-registered **Class 1** device. **CMS does not cover it.** It explicitly funds buyers through "insurance reimbursements, government programs, employer disability programs, corporate sponsorships, service club donations, charitable foundation contributions, crowdsourcing, and other fundraising." **The one clean payer is the VA (100% for qualifying veterans, via FSS/BRCs).** eSight aggressively pursued reimbursement — and the outcome is instructive: **Medicare never covered eSight.** (esighteyewear.com FAQ + Veteran Coverage; lowvisionmd.org eSight FAQ.)
- **IrisVision:** **FDA Class I** registered. **"Not covered by insurance, Medicaid, or Medicare,"** sold via **financing ($125/mo × 36)** and **fully covered by the VA** for qualifying veterans. Same pattern as eSight. IrisVision also pursued reimbursement aggressively and likewise did **not** win Medicare coverage. (irisvision.com FAQ.)
- **OrCam MyEye** (~$4,490, the reading-hardware king that exited vision): "Insurance typically does not cover OrCam." Funded via voc-rehab (job-tied), VA, HSA/FSA, payment plans, and Lions Clubs/charity. (incil.org OrCam; floridareading.com.)
- **Envision Glasses** (~$2,499): same playbook — voc-rehab, VA, out-of-pocket. (floridareading.com.)

**Pattern (VERIFIED):** Every premium vision-wearable company hit the **same wall — no private/Medicare reimbursement** — and routed around it via **VA + voc-rehab + self-pay/financing + charity.** Their "reimbursement" pitch is really a **funding-navigation** service, not a billing code. **This validates our plan to fund via grants/VA/voc-rehab rather than insurance — and warns us not to over-promise "insurance-covered."**

## Q5 — FSA/HSA eligibility

**VERIFIED — YES, with a Letter of Medical Necessity (LMN).**

- Prescribed low-vision aids — magnifiers, CCTVs, screen readers, and similar assistive tech — are **qualified FSA/HSA expenses when paired with an LMN** from the eye-care provider. (nelowvision.com FSA/HSA; fsastore.com eye equipment; withflex.com LMN.)
- **The LMN should tie the device to** safety, activities of daily living, work/school tasks, and reduced caregiver burden, and note why cheaper alternatives failed. Keep an itemized receipt and a usage log. (nelowvision.com; withflex.com.)
- **Caveat:** FSA/HSA spends **the customer's own pre-tax dollars** — it lowers effective cost ~20–35% but is **not** a third-party payer. Useful as a checkout option / marketing point, not a demand driver. **Action: provide a templated LMN our clinical partners can sign — competitors do this.**

## Q6 — Regulatory off-ramp (FDA general wellness) AND the reimbursement tension

**VERIFIED — an obstacle-alert assistive wearable can plausibly be marketed as a low-risk / non-medical product, BUT this trades against insurance billing. The tension is real and must be a deliberate decision.**

- **FDA general-wellness / low-risk policy.** Under FDA's "General Wellness: Policy for Low Risk Devices" (orig. Sept 27, 2019; **revised Jan 2026**), products that are low-risk and make only general-wellness/lifestyle claims — **and do NOT claim to diagnose, cure, mitigate, prevent, or treat a disease** — are outside active FDA device regulation (enforcement discretion / excluded under FD&C Act §520(o)(1)(B)). The Jan-2026 revision mostly clarifies *sensor wearables* (BP/glucose) and **does not specifically address assistive/disability devices** — so it neither blocks nor blesses our framing; we'd rely on the general low-risk + no-disease-claim logic. (FDA general wellness guidance; Covington Jan-2026 summary; innolitics.)
- **How to stay on the off-ramp:** position Second Eye as an **assistive mobility aid** ("alerts you to obstacles in your path") and **avoid medical/diagnostic claims** ("diagnoses/treats blindness," "prevents falls," "FDA-cleared to…"). A white-cane-adjacent travel aid is a strong fit for low-risk. **A formal device determination from FDA/counsel is still UNCONFIRMED — get a regulatory opinion before launch claims.**
- **THE TENSION (most important strategic catch in this report):** Choosing **"assistive, not medical"** *helps* speed/cost to market and matches our positioning — **but most insurance/DME billing pathways REQUIRE medical-device status.** A general-wellness product generally **cannot be billed to Medicare/DME as a covered medical device.**
  - **The reassuring news:** our two best real payers **don't require it the way insurance does.** **The VA** bought eSight/IrisVision as "Prosthetic and Sensory Aids" — eSight is only a **Class 1** registered device (the lowest tier; **Class 1 ≈ general-controls, often 510(k)-exempt**), yet the VA still covers it 100%. **Voc-rehab** funds devices by *employment justification + IPE*, not by HCPCS/medical-device status. So **staying low-risk does NOT block the VA or voc-rehab paths** — the two paths we actually want.
  - **What it DOES cost us:** it forecloses the (already near-dead) private/Medicare DME-billing path. Given Q1/Q4 show that path is a myth for this category anyway, **the tradeoff is favorable: go assistive/low-risk, keep VA + voc-rehab, accept that insurance billing was never realistic.** Consider a **Class 1 FDA registration/listing** (as eSight/IrisVision did) as a low-cost credibility + FSS-eligibility signal **without** claiming to be a high-risk medical device. (UNCONFIRMED: whether VA FSS listing *requires* FDA registration — ask the VA NAC.)

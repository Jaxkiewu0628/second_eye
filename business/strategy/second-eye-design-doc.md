## Problem Statement
A blind or low-vision person walking through the world has no fast, hands-free, offline way to know "what is in front of me and where." A white cane finds things at foot level a half-step before you hit them. A guide dog is expensive and scarce. Phone apps (Seeing AI, Be My Eyes) require holding a phone up and a network connection, and cannot scan your path while you walk. The result is a daily tax of collisions, hesitation, and lost independence.

Second Eye is sub-50g sport-style glasses that run computer vision on the device itself (no internet needed) and speak the result into the user's ears with directional audio. The first job: real-time obstacle and path detection while walking.

## Demand Evidence
Indirect but strong, not yet first-party. To be validated (see The Assignment).
- Incumbents charge $1,700 to $5,000 for assistive vision wearables (OrCam MyEye, Envision, eSight) and still sell, which proves real willingness to pay exists in this category.
- Founder Jackie Wu's earlier version of this exact concept ("YCane") won the #1 Power Pitch Award at the Conrad Challenge 2023, an external panel of expert judges rating the problem and pitch as the most compelling in its category.
- Gap: no paying customer or behavioral demand signal yet for THIS device. That is the point of the research and discovery sprint.

## Status Quo
What a blind user does today to solve "what is in front of me":
- White cane (about $20 to $60): detects ground-level obstacles a half-step ahead, misses anything above waist height (branches, signs, open cabinet doors).
- Guide dog (about $40,000 to train, multi-year waitlists, scarce).
- Phone apps Seeing AI / Be My Eyes / Aira: good for reading text and describing a scene when you stop and point the phone. Useless for continuous walking, and network-dependent.
- Ultrasonic smart canes (WeWALK, about $600): crude above-ground detection, no identification of what the object is.

The workaround is a stack of partial tools and a lot of memorized routes. The cost is collisions, slower travel, and dependence on others.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge
Target human: an independent-minded blind or severely low-vision adult who already travels with a white cane, owns a smartphone, and wants more confidence moving through unfamiliar or cluttered space. Secondary buyer and influencer: family members and O&M (orientation and mobility) instructors.

Narrowest wedge: real-time, hands-free, offline obstacle and path detection with directional audio. The one job a free phone app structurally cannot do. Reading, faces, and product recognition come later as platform expansion, not as the wedge (phones already do those for free).

## Constraints

- Audio-first means NO display, which removes the project's biggest cost and risk (optics).

## Premises
1. The moat is offline, hands-free vision for someone who cannot look at a screen, not the chip or the frame. (Founder's own docs agree.) STILL HOLDS.
2. The validated edge is offline + on-device + cheap + hands-free GLASSES. This is a delivery advantage that applies to BOTH reading and obstacle detection. REVISED from "audio-first, no display is the wedge": no-display keeps us cheap but caps initial market at the truly-blind core (<1M US); low-vision (retains sight, may want a display) is ~3x bigger.
3. WEDGE = MOBILITY as the reason-to-buy, reading/scene as a free bonus (sharpened 2026-06-30 by the competitor teardown; see strategy/competitive-landscape.md). The first research pass found the user survey leaned toward INFORMATION ACCESS (reading #1). The competitor data resolves the tension: reading is commoditized to FREE (Seeing AI, Be My AI, Lookout) and OrCam — the $4,490 reading-hardware king — just COLLAPSED, killed by free phone AI. So reading cannot be the reason to buy hardware. Mobility (real-time obstacle/path detection while walking) is the one job a phone can't do, which is why a funded hardware category exists (NOA, .lumen, Ara, Glide, WeWALK) — demand validated by revealed willingness-to-pay for hardware, not just stated preference. Our stack does OCR too, so ship reading/scene as a free bonus; never pitch it. Discovery question sharpens to: would you pay $400-700 for hardware doing mobility your free phone apps can't, AND can a sub-50g frame detect obstacles well enough outdoors?
4. Bootstrapped changes the funding path. Assistive unlocks non-dilutive grants and vocational-rehab budgets that a sport product could not. STRENGTHENED: VA provides "electronic mobility devices" at no out-of-pocket; voc-rehab funds smart glasses/OCR readers. (Medicare excludes low-vision aids; consumer insurance won't pay.)
5. Reliability is the product, not a feature. 90% reliable equals 0% trusted in a safety market. STRENGTHENED: ~29% assistive-device abandonment rate, driven by lack of user buy-in and poor real-world performance. Co-design with blind users from day one is existential.

User confirmed direction: Path A. User insight: because the prototype "can be done instantly" on Jackie's validated stack, the binding constraint is market validation, so research and discovery come before more hardware work.

## Approaches Considered
### Approach A: Narrow audio wedge, direct-to-consumer (CHOSEN)
Cheapest possible audio-first obstacle glasses, sold direct to blind users and families at roughly $400 versus OrCam's $4,500. Effort M, Risk Med. Reuses the validated on-device YOLO detector and the offline speech-to-text / text-to-speech voice loop almost directly.

### Approach B: Institutional / reimbursed
Same device, sold through vocational rehab, the VA, schools for the blind, and insurers. Effort L, Risk Med-High. Best margins and stickiness, slowest revenue, possible medical-device or reimbursement classification. Used here as the FUNDING mechanism, not the initial GTM.

### Approach C: License the vision module
Sell the edge-vision + offline-voice module to existing assistive players or OEMs. Effort S-M, Risk Med. Fastest revenue but commodity-supplier trap. Held as a hedge if DTC proves too slow.

### Approach D: Platform from day one
Build assistive but architect as a general offline visual co-pilot, expand into cycling/hiking/industrial later. Effort XL, Risk Med. Used here as the ARCHITECTURE horizon, not the day-one scope.

## Recommended Approach
Build A. Fund it like B. Architect it like D. Keep C as the hedge.
Ship an offline, sub-$700, no-subscription assistive-glasses device on Jackie's validated stack. The FEATURE wedge (reading-first vs obstacle-first vs both) is now OPEN and gated on user discovery, not chosen from the desk; current lean is offline reading + scene description as hero with obstacle alerts as the differentiator. Pay for it with non-dilutive assistive grants, VA, and vocational-rehab money. Write the software so the same box can grow into the broader offline co-pilot. If DTC stalls, license the module (C).

## Market Research Findings (2026-06-29, via /deep-research, 111 agents, adversarially verified)
Verdict: CONDITIONAL GO. Validated assets: (1) price/offline/one-time is genuine white space (all incumbents $1,199-$2,499+ or subscription: Envision $2,499, Glide $1,199+$30/mo, Aira $26-1,160/mo); (2) open positioning lane (Envision = reading only, zero obstacle detection; Glide = mobility but handheld robot not glasses); (3) real cane gap at head level; (4) payer path exists (VA + voc-rehab); (5) regulatory manageable (FDA general-wellness off-ramp; Class II precedent; position as assistive not medical). Challenged: (a) mobility-first NOT validated, signal leans information access; (b) market smaller + low-vision-skewed (truly-blind core <1M US); (c) ~29% abandonment, real-world performance + co-design decide viability. Must beat "free phone app (Seeing AI/Be My Eyes) + white cane," not just expensive incumbents. Gaps not yet researched: global WHO sizing, full competitor teardown, the failure graveyard, exact reimbursement codes.

## Open Questions
- Is the painkiller mobility (obstacle) or information access (reading/faces)? This reshapes the whole product. Resolve with users.
- Is an obstacle-avoidance wearable an FDA-regulated medical device, or consumer assistive tech (like OrCam)? Determines timeline and cost.
- What do agencies actually reimburse, and under what codes or programs?
- Real-world reliability target: what miss rate is safe enough to be trusted?
- Bone-conduction vs open-ear speaker vs stereo earbuds for directional audio without blocking ambient sound.
